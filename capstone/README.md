# 🏆 The Capstone — Build Something Real

> **What this is:** the final project of the course. You'll pick one track, spend 10–20
> hours over 2–4 weeks, and finish with something real — made by you, finished by you,
> showable to anyone.
>
> **Why it matters:** everything you've learned so far lives in your head. The capstone
> moves it into the world.

---

## 🧠 Why a capstone?

There's a difference between these two sentences:

> "I took a course about software engineering."
>
> "Look what I made."

The first is a claim. The second is proof. Module 19 was blunt about it: employers,
collaborators, and (most importantly) *you* believe finished things, not certificates.
A capstone converts knowledge into evidence — a link you can send, a page you can show,
a story you can tell in an interview: what you built, where you got stuck, how you got
unstuck.

And there's a quieter reason. Somewhere around Module 05 you may have started to
suspect you could actually *do* this. The capstone is where you find out that you were
right.

## 📏 The rules

**1. Small is beautiful.** A finished-small project beats an abandoned-big one — every
time, in every way. The graveyard of learning-to-code is full of half-built grand
visions. Your capstone should feel slightly *too* modest when you plan it. That's the
correct size.

**2. Use everything from the course.** This is the point of a capstone — the tools stop
being lessons and start being *your tools*:

- **Git** for version history ([Module 08](../modules/08-git-and-github/README.md)) —
  commit as you go; your history *is* part of the deliverable.
- **User stories** to plan ([Module 07](../modules/07-idea-to-app/README.md)) — before
  building anything, write who needs what and why.
- **A test plan** before "shipping" ([Module 09](../modules/09-bugs-and-testing/README.md))
  — try to break your own work before showing it.
- **The [Glossary](../GLOSSARY.md)** when a word goes blurry — that's what it's for.

**3. Budget honestly: 10–20 hours over 2–4 weeks.** In sessions of 1–2 hours. Put the
sessions in your calendar now — the Lab 19 plan has a slot waiting for exactly this.

## 🪜 The getting-unstuck protocol

Work down this ladder, in order. Most problems die on the first two rungs.

1. **Reread.** The instruction, the error message, your own code — slowly, aloud if
   nobody's watching. Half of all bugs are a mismatch between what you wrote and what
   you *think* you wrote.
2. **Search the exact error in quotes.** Copy the exact message into a search engine,
   in quotation marks. Someone has hit your error before. Their thread is waiting.
3. **Rubber duck.** Explain the problem out loud, line by line, to a duck, a pet, or a
   patient spouse (Module 09 taught you why this absurd trick works — explaining forces
   the fuzzy part into the open).
4. **Ask a community, with a good question.** The one you joined in Lab 19. Include:
   what you're trying to do, what you expected, what happened instead (exact error), and
   what you already tried. Questions shaped like that get answers within hours — and
   writing one often reveals the answer before you hit post.

> ⚠️ **Watch out:** the ladder has a hidden fifth rung — **walk away for a day**. It
> feels like quitting. It isn't. Brains debug in the background; the number of bugs
> solved in the shower is not a joke, it's a documented phenomenon.
