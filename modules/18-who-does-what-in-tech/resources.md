# 📚 Resources 18 — Go Deeper on Teams, Roles & Agile

All free. Pick based on how you like to learn — you don't need all of them.

---

## 🌐 Read

- **[Atlassian Agile Coach](https://www.atlassian.com/agile)** — the same friendly guide
  we recommended in Module 07, but now you're ready for its deeper pages: standups,
  retrospectives, kanban vs. scrum, and how boards really get used. Written by the
  company behind Jira — the board software named (once, gently) in this lesson.
  **Best for:** anyone who wants the ritual-by-ritual details, especially managers and
  PMs-to-be.

- **[The Agile Manifesto](https://agilemanifesto.org)** — the original 2001 page: four
  values, twelve principles. Re-read it *after* this module and notice something
  striking: it never mentions standups, sprints, points, or boards. Those are all later
  inventions layered on top of a very human little document. **Best for:** everyone;
  two minutes, oddly moving.

- **["Shape Up" by Basecamp](https://basecamp.com/shapeup)** — a complete, free online
  book from the company Basecamp about how *they* build software — deliberately
  different from sprint-based agile (six-week cycles, no daily standups, small teams
  with unusual freedom). Reading one respected team argue *against* the standard rituals
  is the best proof that "agile" is a philosophy, not a rulebook. **Best for:** readers
  who finished this module thinking "do all teams really work this way?" (Answer: no —
  and this book is the famous counterexample.)

- **[levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi)** — the well-known site where tech workers
  share real salaries by role, company, and level. Useful here for a different reason
  than money: browse it to see how the roles from this lesson (engineer, EM, PM,
  designer, data scientist…) ladder up through levels at real companies — it makes the
  org chart concrete. **Best for:** career-changers and the curious; take individual
  numbers with a grain of salt, patterns matter more than any one data point.

## 🖼️ Browse

- **[Wizard Zines by Julia Evans](https://wizardzines.com)** — famous, friendly,
  hand-drawn mini-magazines ("zines") explaining real tech topics with cartoons and
  plain words. Julia Evans is a working engineer beloved for making hard things
  approachable — very much this course's spirit, drawn instead of written. Some zines
  cost money; her site also links to a large collection of free comics and her free
  weekly posts. **Best for:** visual learners, and anyone who wants proof that serious
  engineers value plain explanations as much as you do.

## 🔍 A note on searching for more

If you search "agile", you will find an industry of certifications, frameworks with
trademarked names, and consultants disagreeing loudly. Two filters that help: prefer
material that describes what real teams *do* (like the resources above) over material
that sells a certified method; and remember the lesson's core — a wish-list, short
cycles, a daily huddle, and blameless reflection. Anything much more complicated than
that is decoration, and often expensive decoration.
