**1. The lesson says a computer has three "superpowers". What are they?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

It is extremely **fast** (billions of instructions per second), extremely **obedient**
(never skips a step, never gets bored), and extremely **literal** (does exactly what the
instructions say — including the mistakes, because it never guesses what you meant).
</details>

**2. Why do computers use on/off switches (binary) instead of, say, switches with ten positions?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Reliability. Electricity is messy — voltages wobble. But "on or off?" stays a crisp,
unmistakable question even when conditions aren't perfect. Two clean states are far more
dependable than ten fuzzy ones.
</details>

**3. What is a bit? And how many bits are in a byte?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

A **bit** is one single 0-or-1 — the smallest piece of information a computer can hold,
like one light switch. Eight bits make one **byte** (roughly enough to store one letter
of text).
</details>

**4. A computer can only store numbers. So how does it store the word "HI"?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

By agreement, every letter has a number (in the standard code, H is 72 and I is 73).
The computer stores those numbers in binary. "Everything is numbers": letters, photos
(color values per dot), and sound (pressure measurements) all become numbers first.
</details>

**5. Match each computer part to its kitchen role: CPU, RAM, storage, motherboard, GPU.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

- **CPU** → the chef (carries out every instruction, one after another, very fast)
- **RAM** → the kitchen counter (fast to reach, limited space, wiped clean when power
  goes off)
- **Storage** → the pantry / filing cabinet (slow to reach, huge, keeps everything even
  without power)
- **Motherboard** → the building's wiring and plumbing (connects everything, does no
  cooking itself)
- **GPU** → a thousand junior chefs (many simple calculations, all at the same time)
</details>

**6. Your friend's laptop died mid-sentence and their unsaved document vanished — but all
their old photos are fine. Explain both facts with one idea from the lesson.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The document was in **RAM** (the counter), which loses everything when power is cut. The
photos were in **storage** (the pantry), which keeps its contents without power. Saving
a file means copying it from the counter to the pantry.
</details>

**7. Why does "turn it off and on again" fix so many problems?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Hours of running leave RAM cluttered with leftover temporary state — stuck programs,
piled-up small errors. Powering off wipes RAM completely (it can't hold anything without
power), so the machine restarts with a clean counter. Files in storage are untouched.
It fixes temporary messes, not permanent problems.
</details>

**8. True or false: a smartphone is a real computer.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

True. It has a CPU, RAM, storage, a GPU, and input/output devices — the full kitchen.
It is also far more powerful than the computers that navigated astronauts to the Moon.
"Computer" in this course always includes your phone.
</details>

**9. A laptop ad says: "16 GB RAM, 512 GB SSD." Which number is the counter, which is
the pantry — and why is the pantry number so much bigger?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

16 GB RAM is the counter; 512 GB SSD is the pantry. The pantry is bigger because fast
memory is expensive to make, while slower permanent storage is cheap — so machines pair
a small fast workspace with a large slow warehouse.
</details>

**10. What is Moore's law, in one sentence?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

The observation (by Gordon Moore, 1965) that engineers fit roughly twice as many
switches on a chip every couple of years — a doubling that compounded for decades and
explains why a pocket phone now outpowers a room-sized 1970s computer, though the pace
has been slowing as switches approach the size of atoms.
</details>

**11. Why is a GPU better than a CPU for video games (and for AI)?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Games and AI both need *millions of small, similar calculations at the same time* —
like updating every pixel on screen 60 times a second. A CPU is one masterful chef
working through tasks in order; a GPU is a thousand junior chefs each doing one simple
task simultaneously. For huge piles of simple parallel work, the thousand juniors win.
</details>

**12. 🗣️ Explain it to a friend.** Imagine a friend asks: *"OK, so what actually IS a
computer? What's inside my phone?"* Give your answer out loud, in under two minutes,
using at least one analogy from this module. No fold to open this time — if you can say
it so your friend nods along, you've passed.

<details><summary>Stuck? Here's one way to start</summary>

"Your phone is a machine that follows instructions — billions per second, totally
literally, never guessing. Inside, everything is stored as patterns of tiny on/off
switches. Think of it as a kitchen: there's a chef (the processor) doing the work, a
counter (memory) holding what it's working on right now — which gets wiped when the
power's off, that's why restarting fixes things — and a pantry (storage) where your
photos and apps live permanently…"
</details>
