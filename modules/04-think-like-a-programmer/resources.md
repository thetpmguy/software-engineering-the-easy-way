# 📚 Resources 04 — Go Deeper on Thinking Like a Programmer

All free. All optional. Pick what matches how you like to learn — none of these are homework.

> **⚠️ Watch out:** websites reorganize their pages from time to time. We link only to famous, stable homepages and tell you what to search for once you're there. If a page has moved, the site's own search box will find it.

---

## 🎮 Interactive (learn by doing)

- **Hour of Code — [code.org](https://code.org)**
  Free, browser-based, hour-long coding activities using drag-and-drop blocks. No sign-up needed to play. Best for: absolute beginners who want to *feel* loops and decisions before reading any more theory. (This is Exercise 4 of the [lab](lab.md) — if you skipped it, it's worth the hour.)

- **Scratch — [scratch.mit.edu](https://scratch.mit.edu)**
  MIT's free block-based programming playground. You snap colorful blocks together to make animations and games — every block is a step in an algorithm, and loops and decisions are literal puzzle pieces. Best for: playful learners, and anyone who wants more after Hour of Code. Built for kids, genuinely great for adults; no account needed to experiment.

## 📖 Reading

- **BBC Bitesize: Computational Thinking — [bbc.co.uk/bitesize](https://www.bbc.co.uk/bitesize)**
  Short, friendly written guides with diagrams covering decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, and algorithms — the exact four moves from this module. On the homepage, search for "computational thinking." Best for: readers who want a second explanation of this lesson in different words, with quick self-tests.

## 🎬 Video

- **Crash Course Computer Science — search "Crash Course Computer Science" on YouTube**
  A famous, fast, funny 40-episode series. For this module, look for the episodes on *algorithms* (roughly episodes 13–15 in the series list): what algorithms are and how the same problem can be solved in faster or slower ways. Best for: people who like energetic 12-minute videos with animations. Don't worry if some episodes race ahead of you — future modules of this course cover that material.

## How to use these

If you do only one thing: spend a rainy afternoon on **Scratch** and try to build the tea-making algorithm from the lab as an animation. Making a cat *actually follow your steps* teaches exactness better than any page of reading — including this one.

When you're ready, move on to [Module 05 — Your First Code](../05-your-first-code/README.md).
